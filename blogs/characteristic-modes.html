Characteristic Mode Analysis (CMA)

Technical Blog · Theory & Design Methodology
Characteristic Mode Analysis (CMA) has transitioned from a theoretical curiosity in the 1970s to a cornerstone of modern antenna design, particularly for 5G MIMO and platform-integrated systems. This document details the mathematical derivation from the Method of Moments (MoM), physical interpretation of modal parameters, and systematic design methodologies.

1. Mathematical Foundation: From EFIE to Eigenvalues

The theory, refined by Harrington and Mautz, begins with the Electric Field Integral Equation (EFIE).

1.1 The Impedance Operator

For a perfectly conducting body, the tangential electric field is zero:

[L(J) − Ei]tan = 0

The operator L relates the surface current J to the scattered field. Using the Method of Moments (MoM), this is discretized into a matrix equation:

[Z][I] = [V]

Where [Z] = [R] + j[X] is the complex impedance matrix.

1.2 The Weighted Eigenvalue Equation

CMA solves for the eigencurrents Jn that diagonalize both the radiation resistance (R) and reactance (X) matrices:

[X] Jn = λn [R] Jn
Key Property: The resulting eigencurrents are real and orthogonal over the surface, and their far-fields are orthogonal over a sphere at infinity.

2. Physical Insight via Modal Parameters

Unlike standard S-parameter analysis, CMA provides parameters that describe the inherent capability of a structure to radiate.

2.1 Eigenvalues (λn)

The eigenvalue represents the ratio of stored reactive power to radiated power.

ConditionPhysical Meaning
λn = 0Resonance. Optimal radiation efficiency.
λn > 0Inductive. Magnetic energy dominates (stored energy).
λn < 0Capacitive. Electric energy dominates (stored energy).

2.2 Modal Significance (MS) — Deep Dive

The Modal Significance (MS) is a normalized parameter derived from the eigenvalue λn that quantifies how effectively a specific characteristic mode can radiate at a given frequency.

MSn = | 1 / (1 + jλn) |

Normalization Range

MS normalizes the eigenvalue λn, which can range from −∞ to +∞, into a strictly bounded range of 0 to 1:

ConditionPhysical Meaning
MS = 1Occurs when λn = 0. The mode is at resonance and has the maximum potential to radiate.
MS → 0Occurs as |λn| → ∞. The mode is non-resonant and stores significant reactive energy, making it a very poor radiator.

High MS and Radiation Efficiency

A "high" MS value is typically defined as MS ≥ 0.707. This threshold is significant for several reasons:

Ease of Excitation: Modes with MS ≥ 0.707 are considered "significant" because they can be easily excited by an external source (like a feed) to contribute to the total radiation of the antenna.
Power Balance: At MS = 0.707 (where |λn| = 1), the power radiated by the mode is exactly equal to the net reactive power it stores.

Tracking Bandwidth

MS is a powerful tool for tracking the potential bandwidth of a structure before a physical feed is even designed.

MS CharacteristicInterpretation
MS curve widthThe "radiating bandwidth" of a mode is defined by the frequency range over which its MS remains above the 0.707 threshold.
Narrow, sharp peakHigh-Q, narrowband mode — the mode resonates strongly but over a very limited frequency range.
Broad MS curveLow-Q, wideband mode — the mode can contribute to radiation over a wide frequency range.

2.3 Characteristic Angle (αn)

Describes the phase angle between the modal current and field:

αn = 180° − tan−1(λn)

Resonance occurs at 180°. This parameter is vital for predicting how multiple modes will combine in wideband designs.

3. Advanced Design Methodologies

3.1 Systematic Feed Placement

By examining the Modal Current Distribution, designers can place feeds (probes, slots, or coupling elements) at current maxima to strongly excite a mode, or at nulls to suppress it.

3.2 Platform Integration and MIMO

For devices like smartphones, CMA treats the entire chassis as the radiator.

MIMO Isolation: Since characteristic modes are inherently orthogonal, exciting different modes (e.g., a dipole-like mode and a loop-like mode) provides high isolation without physical separation.

4. CMA for Non-Metallic Structures

CMA can and does work for non-metallic structures, but the reason it isn't formulated using "direct" Maxwell differential equations (like FDTD or FEM solvers) comes down to a fundamental mathematical requirement: the separation of radiated and stored power.

4.1 Why Direct Maxwell (Differential Form) is Difficult for CMA

Standard Maxwell solvers like those in CST (Transient) or HFSS (Driven) solve the differential form of Maxwell's equations within a bounded box.

The "Boundary" Problem: CMA requires a clear mathematical distinction between the energy that stays near the object (stored) and the energy that escapes to infinity (radiated). Differential equations require artificial absorbing boundary conditions (like PML) to simulate "infinity". Because these boundaries are numerical approximations, they "pollute" the eigenvalue calculation, making it nearly impossible to get the clean, real-valued eigenvalues needed for Modal Significance.

4.2 The Solution: Integral Equations for Non-Metals

To analyze dielectrics (glass, plastic, ceramics), engineers use Integral Equation (IE) versions of Maxwell’s equations instead of differential ones:

MethodDescription
VIEVolume Integral Equation — treats the dielectric as a cloud of "equivalent volume currents". Physically accurate for inhomogeneous materials but computationally heavy (requires meshing the entire 3D volume).
SIE / PMCHWTSurface Integral Equation — the "gold standard" for homogeneous dielectrics (like a Dielectric Resonator Antenna). Uses the PMCHWT formulation (Poggio, Miller, Chang, Harrington, Wu, Tsai), placing equivalent electric and magnetic currents only on the surface.

4.3 How it Works for Dielectrics

When you run CMA on a non-metal structure using these formulations, you get:

Electric Currents (J): Representing the displacement currents in the material.
Magnetic Currents (M): Representing the change in magnetic flux at the boundaries.

These modes still give you Modal Significance and Characteristic Angles, allowing you to design "Antennas without Metal," such as Dielectric Resonator Antennas (DRAs), by exciting the natural dielectric modes.

Summary of CMA Availability

Material TypeSolver TechnologyKey Formulation
Metals (PEC)Surface Integral (MoM)EFIE (Electric Field Integral Eq)
Homogeneous DielectricsSurface Integral (MoM)PMCHWT (Surface currents)
Complex / InhomogeneousVolume Integral (VIE)Volume equivalent currents

5. Characteristic Angle: Metal Plate vs. Dielectric Sphere

The Characteristic Angle (αn) provides a phase-based view of how a mode transitions from storing energy to radiating it. While both a metal plate and a dielectric sphere can resonate, their angle plots behave differently.

Comparison Table

FeatureMetal (PEC) PlateDielectric Sphere (DRA)
Resonanceαn = 180°αn = 180°
Angle RangeStrictly 90° < αn < 270°Can exhibit "looping" or rapid phase shifts
Slope at ResonanceTypically follows an "S-curve" shapeSteeper slopes due to higher Q-factors
Mode TypeSurface currents only (J)Equivalent surface (J, M) or volume currents

Metal Plate (PEC) Behavior

Capacitive Region (90° < α < 180°): At low frequencies, the plate behaves like a capacitor. The angle starts near 90° and rises toward 180° as frequency increases.
Inductive Region (180° < α < 270°): After passing resonance (180°), the mode becomes inductive and the angle continues toward 270°.

For a PEC plate, the modes are relatively "broad," meaning the angle changes gradually with frequency, indicating a lower Q-factor and wider potential bandwidth.

Dielectric Sphere Behavior

High Q Resonances: Dielectric spheres trap energy much more effectively than open metal plates. This results in very steep characteristic angle curves that "snap" through the 180° resonance point over a very narrow frequency range.
Internal vs. External Fields: Because the dielectric has a specific permittivity (εr), the angle plot is heavily influenced by internal standing waves. Higher-order modes appear much more frequently compared to a metal plate of similar size.
Magnetic Modes: Unlike the plate, a sphere easily supports TE (Transverse Electric) and TM (Transverse Magnetic) modes. You will often see two distinct sets of angle curves: one for electric-type resonances and one for magnetic-type resonances.

Key Takeaway: The "steepness" of the characteristic angle slope is a direct indicator of the stored energy in the structure. Dielectric spheres concentrate fields internally, leading to rapid phase transitions.
