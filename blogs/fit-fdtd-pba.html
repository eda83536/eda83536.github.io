Finite Integration Technique (FIT), FDTD & Perfect Boundary Approximation

Technical Blog · White Paper · Comparative Study
The Finite Integration Technique (FIT) represents a paradigm shift from differential-based methods like FDTD. By discretizing the integral form of Maxwell’s equations, FIT provides a robust, physically consistent framework for simulating complex electromagnetic phenomena. This document explores the mathematical foundations, the PBA revolution, and comparative analysis with FEM and MoM.

1. Discretizing Maxwell: Two Different Paths

The core challenge in Computational Electromagnetics (CEM) is converting Maxwell’s continuous partial differential equations into a discrete form. Volume-based solvers accomplish this by dividing the entire simulation space into small cells.

1.1 FDTD: Differential Approximation

Introduced by Kane Yee in 1966, FDTD discretizes the differential form of Maxwell’s equations. The "Yee-grid" places electric (E) and magnetic (H) field components at staggered locations within a Cartesian cell, using "Central Difference" approximations for derivatives.

Staircasing Limitation: Because FDTD relies on point-to-point differences, if a material boundary doesn’t align with the grid, the solver must "snap" it to the nearest edge. Smooth curves become jagged steps, leading to artificial numerical scattering and frequency shifts.

1.2 FIT: Integral Consistency

Proposed by Thomas Weiland in 1977, FIT starts from the integral form of Maxwell’s equations. Instead of approximating a derivative at a single point, FIT integrates fields along edges and over faces of a dual-grid system (G and Ĩ).

The Maxwell-Grid-Equations (MGE)

FIT transforms the four Maxwell equations into discrete matrix equations:

C e = −d/dt b    (Faraday’s Law)
Ĉ h = d/dt d + j    (Ampere’s Law)
S b = 0    (Gauss’s Law — Magnetic)
Ĩ d = q    (Gauss’s Law — Electric)
Key Advantage: The topology (how cells connect) is separated from the geometry (lengths and areas). Material properties are contained in "Material Matrices" (Mε, Mμ, Mσ), linking fields on primary and dual grids. Because FIT uses integrals, it naturally enforces Gauss’s Law and the non-existence of magnetic monopoles across the entire grid.

2. The PBA Revolution: Solving the Staircase Problem

The defining feature of modern FIT (as implemented in CST Studio Suite) is Perfect Boundary Approximation (PBA).

2.1 Conformal Mapping

Traditional FDTD treats a cell as entirely filled or entirely empty. PBA allows a cell to be partially filled:

How it works: PBA calculates the exact intersection of a CAD surface with mesh cell edges. It then adjusts the "filling factor" of the permittivity and permeability tensors for that specific cell. The integration path follows the actual curve, not the mesh line.

The result: a conformal representation within a Cartesian grid. You can model a perfect circle or a complex curved car body without millions of tiny "staircase" cells. FIT with PBA achieves the same accuracy as FDTD but with a mesh that is often 10–20x coarser.

2.2 Thin Sheet Technique (TST)

An extension of PBA, TST allows the solver to model sheets thinner than a single mesh cell (like a PCB trace or a carbon fiber panel) without requiring a mesh fine enough to resolve the thickness.

4.1 Why Not Always Use FEM?

FEM uses tetrahedral meshes that "shrink-wrap" complex shapes accurately. However, it requires solving massive systems of linear equations (Ax = b). As mesh cells (N) grow, the cost of matrix inversion increases non-linearly (O(N²) to O(N³)). FIT/FDTD is explicit — it updates fields cell-by-cell with linear O(N) scaling, making it the only viable choice for electrically large structures.

4.2 Why Not Always Use MoM?

MoM only meshes metal surfaces (no air mesh), making it fast for antennas in open space. However, it creates a dense matrix where every element "talks" to every other element. For complex dielectrics (human body, multi-layered plastic housing), MoM becomes impractical. FIT/FDTD handles inhomogeneous materials naturally — it’s the "gold standard" for Consumer Electronics and Bio-EM (SAR) simulations.

6. Practical Implications

Simulation Speed: By using PBA, an engineer can use 5–10x fewer mesh cells than a staircase FDTD solver for the same level of accuracy.
Broadband Performance: A single FIT run with a Gaussian pulse provides the entire S-parameter spectrum (S₁₁, S₂₁) from DC to the cutoff frequency.
Memory Efficiency: Memory scales linearly O(N) with mesh cells, making it superior to FEM O(N²) for electrically large structures.
The "Integration Solver" (FIT) combines the computational speed of FDTD with the geometric accuracy of conformal methods through PBA. Its "Universal Discretization" allows seamless transitions between time and frequency domain analysis.
