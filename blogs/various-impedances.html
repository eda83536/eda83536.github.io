Various Impedances in RF & EM Engineering

Technical Blog · Reference Guide
A comprehensive reference of impedance types encountered in electromagnetics, antenna design, transmission line theory, and EMC. Each impedance captures a different physical relationship between voltage, current, and electromagnetic fields.
Impedance TypeDefinition & Engineering Context
Intrinsic Impedance ηA fundamental property of a material medium, representing the ratio of the electric field to the magnetic field for a plane wave. In a vacuum, this is approximately 377 Ω. It depends solely on the material's permeability and permittivity, describing how the medium itself resists the electromagnetic wave regardless of any physical boundaries.
Characteristic Impedance Z₀The ratio of voltage to current for a single wave propagating along a uniform transmission line (like a coax or microstrip). It is determined by the physical geometry and materials of the line. If a line is terminated with this value, it behaves as if it is infinitely long, preventing any signal reflections.
Wave Impedance ZwThe ratio of the transverse electric field to the transverse magnetic field at a specific point in a waveguide or antenna system. Unlike intrinsic impedance, wave impedance depends on the specific "mode" of travel (TE or TM) and the frequency, often changing significantly near the waveguide's cutoff frequency.
System Impedance ZsysA standardized reference value (usually 50 Ω or 75 Ω) chosen for an entire ecosystem of hardware to ensure compatibility. By designing all connectors, cables, and components to this single value, engineers can plug different devices together without needing custom matching for every connection.
Input Impedance ZinThe total impedance "seen" by a source looking into a transmission line or component port. It is a combination of the line's characteristic impedance, its electrical length, and the actual load at the other end. It changes as you move along a mismatched line due to the presence of standing waves.
Surface Impedance ZsUsed to describe the behavior of lossy conductors at high frequencies where the "skin effect" occurs. It relates the tangential electric field at the conductor's surface to the linear current density. It includes both a resistive part (power loss/heat) and an inductive part (stored magnetic energy inside the conductor).
Radiation Impedance ZradThe impedance presented by an antenna to its feedline, consisting of radiation resistance (energy turned into waves) and loss resistance (energy turned into heat). The reactive part represents energy stored in the antenna's near-field. Matching this to the feedline is critical for efficient signal transmission.
Mutual Impedance ZijA measure of the electromagnetic coupling between two nearby components, such as two elements in an antenna array. It describes the voltage induced in one element due to the current flowing in the other. It is essential for calculating the true input impedance of antennas in close proximity.
Transfer Impedance ZtA parameter used in Electromagnetic Compatibility (EMC) to evaluate the effectiveness of cable shielding. It relates the current flowing on the outside of a shield to the voltage induced on the center conductors inside. A lower transfer impedance indicates a higher quality shield that blocks interference more effectively.
Even/Odd Mode ImpedanceSpecific to "coupled" transmission lines (like differential pairs on a PCB). Even-mode impedance occurs when both lines carry identical signals in phase, while odd-mode occurs when signals are 180° out of phase. These are vital for designing high-speed digital buses and directional couplers.
Free Space Impedance η₀A physical constant representing the intrinsic impedance of a perfect vacuum. Defined as the square root of the ratio of vacuum permeability to vacuum permittivity, it is exactly 120π Ω (≈ 376.73 Ω). It serves as the baseline for all wave propagation calculations in air or space.
Normalized Impedance zA dimensionless mathematical value calculated by dividing an actual impedance (Z) by the system's characteristic impedance (Z₀). This technique allows engineers to plot and analyze complex circuits on a Smith Chart regardless of whether the system is 50 Ω, 75 Ω, or any other standard.
Image Impedance ZiUsed primarily in filter design and cascaded networks. It is the impedance that, when connected to the ports of a network, results in the same impedance being seen looking into the opposite port. This ensures that multiple identical stages can be linked together without causing internal reflections.
Terminal ImpedanceThe impedance measured at the specific physical boundaries or "terminals" of a component. In multi-port network analysis (Z-parameters), it defines how the voltage at one port relates to currents at all other ports, helping describe the behavior of a "black box" circuit within a larger system.
Aperture ImpedanceDescribes the "resistance" an electromagnetic field encounters when passing through an opening (like a slot or hole) in a conducting surface. It is used in the study of diffraction and shielding effectiveness, quantifying how much energy leaks through a gap in a microwave housing or enclosure.
